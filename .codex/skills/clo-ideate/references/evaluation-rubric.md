# Ideation Evaluation Rubric

Use this rubric to stress-test a candidate question before discovery.

## Seven Dimensions

### 1. Question Clarity
- can the idea be written as a crisp research question?
- is the outcome, treatment, or phenomenon identifiable?

### 2. Novelty And Closest-Paper Threat
- what is the nearest published or working paper?
- does this idea clearly differ in question, setting, design, data, or mechanism?
- could a skeptical reader say "this is already done"?

### 3. Importance And Nugget Strength
- is there a real stake beyond personal curiosity?
- is the contribution explainable in a few sentences?

### 4. Timing And Frontier Crowding
- why now?
- is the frontier moving in a way that makes this timely or crowded?

### 5. Comparative Advantage
- what makes this team specifically able to do this?
- data access, institutional knowledge, language, setting familiarity, method fit, or unusual source material

### 6. Identification And Data Traction
- is there at least a plausible empirical path?
- is the variation, comparison, or design sketch credible enough to justify discovery work?
- is there at least a plausible data route?

### 7. Fast-Fail Tractability
- what is the cheapest informative next test?
- can the idea be killed or strengthened quickly?

## Verdict Scale

| Range | Verdict | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| `>= 80` | `pursue` | strong enough to move into discovery now |
| `65-79` | `refine` | promising, but one or two major weaknesses should be fixed first |
| `50-64` | `park` | interesting but not worth immediate discovery effort |
| `< 50` | `kill` | too weak or too crowded for the current pipeline |

## Minimum Output Format

Every evaluated idea should report:

- score by dimension
- overall verdict
- strongest objection
- best pivot
- recommended next step

## Economics-Specific Rule

For empirical ideas, a high score is not enough if identification and data traction are both vague.

If the question sounds interesting but the empirical path is missing, cap the verdict at `refine`.
